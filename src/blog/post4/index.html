gpt-2-simple: Training Your Own AI With Machine Learning

17/04/2020

Despite the prominence of machine learning and other forms of artificial intelligence in media over the last several years, it’s still largely inaccessible to everyday users and technology enthusiasts alike. Right now, machine learning is either being used by governments around the world in various Orwellian schemes or by major corporations in their never-ending attempt to sell us something (or to improve your phone’s voice assistant, if you’re lucky). This post helps you unleash the power of machine learning—serving as a step-by-step guide for everything along the way.

Perhaps you’ve seen those tweets about bots writing movie scripts. Maybe you’ve played around with AI Dungeon, a text adventure game that uses (surprise, surprise) AI. More likely, you were slightly confused at the media hype surrounding the AI “too dangerous to release”. Disregarding any emotional trauma from avid Batman fans reading the script, it’s hard to see how something that only generates fake text can be “dangerous” (hint: fake news and scam emails). These scenarios all have something in common. All the computer programs involved use a language model, which is something that gives you the probability of a word appearing next in a text sequence. It’s how your brain realizes you’ve misheard something, and it’s behind the “predictive text” feature in your keyboard. So how is it used to generate text, and how can you “train” it?

I won’t get into most of the technical details, but here’s a brief overview of how most language models work. You input a lot of text (sometimes gigabytes of plain text), and the model splits it up into “tokens”, individual words or pieces of punctuation. It then counts how often a word appears after another word (for example, “example” often appears after “for”), how often a word appears after two other words (for example, “a” often follows “once upon”), how often a word appears after three other words, and so on. A string of words commonly found together is called an n-gram, with n being the number of words. After the language model calculates all this, you can input any word or sentence and get a list of probabilities for which word appears after that. Predictive text just starts with a prompt, picks the most likely word to appear after that, and repeats. But what if you wanted to generate longer, more coherent text?

To use machine learning to generate text, you start with a prompt. Then the AI picks the most likely words from the language model, but slightly randomizes it so it doesn’t always generate the same sentences. As machine learning is self-learning by definition, when you train your AI it compares the generated text to the prompt and human-written texts and adjusts itself to sound more coherent and human-like. Finally, you can “bias” your language model to change its style of writing by feeding it different pieces of text in the beginning. If you want it to sound like J. K. Rowling, you feed it the Harry Potter books. If you want it to write blog posts for you (this is written by me, I promise) you feed it your previous blog posts. If you want a Shakespearean parody, you feed it Shakespeare (all of his plays are available as one text file here). The saying “you are what you eat” definitely goes double for language models!

Today, we’re going to be training the GPT-2 language model. A smaller (and less powerful) version of the model was first released in February 2019, since the larger versions were famously deemed “too dangerous to release” by AI researchers. Nevertheless, the researchers relented and the full version was released in November 2019. It comes in four sizes (small, medium, large, and extra large), but we’ll be using the two smallest sizes because they’re faster and easier to work with. The “small” sized model has a 79.3% accuracy compared to the extra large model, and the medium model has a 91.8% accuracy.

Although we need a moderately powerful GPU (at minimum) to work with the “small” model, we can use Google Colaboratory instead. It allows you to access very powerful (and expensive) GPUs and run Python code on them, all within your browser. It’s primarily used by students and researchers all around the world for AI-related tasks, such as machine/deep learning frameworks, training models, and developing applications that utilize AI.
