Line by Line (Part 2): Improving Your New Website

01/05/2020

By now, you should have gotten your website up and running with Part 1 of the guide. You may notice it looks a little sparse, but that can be easily fixed by adding some HTML (for the content) and CSS (to style it). But how can you make your website stand out by going above and beyond? This part of the guide suggests 10 ways for you to improve your website. The suggestions all come with a difficulty rating out of 5 (5 is the most difficult) but aren’t in any sort of order. Let me know what works for you and what doesn’t. Enjoy!

Think of a clear purpose (2/5)

When you create anything, it’s best to have a clear purpose. For example, when you write a book, you know your target audience, your topic, the style of the book, and what you’re going to put in it. In the same way, having a clear purpose for your website helps you think of ideas for content, and what you want your finished website to look like. Start with a target audience, decide what appeals to that audience, and add some content.

Adding Encryption (3/5)

Encryption is what keeps the internet traffic from your website to visitors secret, and vice versa. If a site is encrypted it’ll have a “green lock” icon next to it in the URL bar, and the URL will begin with “https”. By adding a form of encryption known as Transport Layer Security (TLS) to your website, your visitors can rest easy knowing their data is protected. Your website even ranks higher in search results, too! Try using Google to find specific instructions from your hosting provider (instructions for NameCheap are here).

Hosting something else on your server (4/5)

You currently have a VPS (droplet) for your website, but that’s not all it can do. Only hosting a website on it wastes a lot of computing resources (even for the cheapest tier), so it’s best to host another service on it as well. Some things you can try are hosting 2 websites on the same server, helping with disease research, hosting your own VPN, using it as a seedbox for torrenting, or as your own private cloud storage.

Submit your website to search engines (1/5)

If you want others to find your website, the smartest thing to do would be to submit it to a few different search engines. If you don’t manually submit, the search engines would only notice your website after a month or two. One service that offers free one-click submission to many different search engines is entireweb, available here.

Adding Cloudflare (4/5)

Cloudflare is the world’s most popular Content Delivery Network (CDN). What’s a CDN, you ask? A CDN helps visitors load your site quicker. Instead of getting the website from your servers, your servers pass the website to a network of servers around the world (a CDN) ahead of time, and the visitor loads the site from the server network. Not only are CDNs great for speed, they also provide additional protection against bots (with automated captchas), allow you to restrict who can visit your website, and improve security by protecting your website against DDoS attacks.

Adding a CSS framework/theme (4/5)

As mentioned earlier, CSS “styles” your website, controlling how it looks and feels. You may not be able to make stunning websites from scratch with a basic knowledge of CSS, but there are many CSS frameworks and themes available online that you can adapt to your liking. Bootstrap is one such CSS framework, and there are many templates available here.

Use server-side scripting to interact with the user (5/5)

If you want to change your website based on user input, your best bet is to use server-side scripting. It’s a group of programming languages that allow you to get user input, process it, and change your website based on that (including PHP, Perl, and Python). If you’re on a website and it changes (think search engines, weather websites, and social media), it probably has some form of this. However, if you only want animations and such, you can use client-side scripting instead.

Adding regular content (3/5)

Your website may be cool and all, but most people don’t visit a website again if it doesn’t change. Adding regular content helps you get return visitors, whether that be a blog, videos, artwork, or something else. This also ties in with the first suggestion “think of a clear purpose”, because you can only decide which content to add if you have a purpose for your website. If your website’s purpose is to serve as an “online resume”, consider adding some projects. If it’s for your photography work, consider adding some photographs regularly. If it’s about technology, you might want to start a blog :)

Link your social media profiles (1/5)

If you want the world to know who you are, it’s a good idea to put your social media profiles on your website. Consider putting an email address, your Linkedin, your Github, or your other social media profiles on your website. Just be wary of putting your main personal email address on your website; there are a lot of spambots crawling the web, looking for emails.

Promote your website (2/5)

This suggestion is very similar to the previous one, and for good reason. If you want visitors to your website to know about your social media, shouldn’t you also want visitors to your social media to know about your website? Once you have some content on your website, you can (and should) promote it on social media. Along with promotion, you can also actively ask for ideas from others and use them to improve your website (giving due credit, of course).
