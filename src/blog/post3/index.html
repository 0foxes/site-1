Barely Afloat - The Royal Canadian Navy in 2020

08/04/2020

Disregarding Trump’s newly formed US Space Force, there are three ways a country may be attacked or its sovereignty challenged: by land, air, or sea. Despite Canada being at peace today, there have been times in the past when Canadians have had to wage war, either in faraway lands or closer to home. Since Canada is surrounded by three oceans and only borders the US by land (they haven’t tried to invade us since 1812), this burden mainly falls on the air force and the navy. As aircraft ranges are extremely limited (the CF-18 (our primary fighter) has a maximum combat range of just above 500km), Canada’s defence primarily falls to its Navy. In this post, I’m going to be talking about the history, current mission, and future plans of the Royal Canadian Navy (RCN).

First, let’s start with some history. Even though Confederation was in 1867, Britain was responsible for our naval defense until 1910, when the Naval Service was established (it was renamed to the RCN in 1911). Britain equipped our new navy with two outdated ships, and five more were commissioned during WW1. During WW1, the RCN patrolled the east and west coasts of Canada, protecting ships and convoys against German submarines.

The RCN shrank significantly during the interwar years because of a lack of funding, but this was also when the first ships specifically designed for the RCN were built. During WW2, the RCN expanded to many times its original size, escorting convoys all over the Atlantic, fighting in Europe, the Atlantic ocean, the Mediterranean sea, the Pacific ocean, and the Arctic ocean. Finally, the RCN played a significant role in the D-Day landings at Normandy, France. By the end of the war, the RCN was the third-largest navy in the world.

In the winter of 1949, three almost simultaneous “incidents” happened aboard RCN ships. Within the span of a month, large portions of crews on three different ships refused to work altogether unless the captain met with them and heard their grievances. The Canadian government feared communist influence, and they appointed Rear-Admiral Rollo Mainguy to investigate. The famous Mainguy Report concluded that there was no communist influence or collusion between the three crews, and blamed the protests on the absence of a distinct Canadian naval identity.

During the Cold War, the RCN supported the US, the UK, and by extension NATO in a variety of places. In the 1950s and 1960s, the RCN fought in the Korean War (primarily bombarding shore positions), and participated in peacekeeping operations during the Suez crisis. Many of the WW2 ships still in service were retired during this time. In the 1970s and 1980s, the RCN constructed the Iroquois-class destroyers (which were retired recently) and the Halifax-class frigates (which are still in service today). In the 1990s and 2000s, the RCN was involved in Operation Desert Shield, Operation Desert Storm, peacekeeping patrols during the Yugoslav wars, and the international “War on Terror”. More recently, the RCN has been contributing to international operations against piracy in the Arabian sea.
