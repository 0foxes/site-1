Folding@Home: Curing Coronavirus With Your Computer

24/03/2020

With an ever-increasing percentage of the world population under quarantine by the day (whether voluntarily or government-mandated), it's hard to avoid feeling a little bit helpless as scientists, doctors, and researchers around the world scramble to come up with treatments, vaccines, and models for the new coronavirus (COVID-19). What if you could do more than just stay at home? What if there was a way for you to actually help the scientists? There is.

Before we get into how to help, let's take a quick look at what distributed computing is. Essentially, distributed computing is a computer system that runs over multiple computers. Those different computers are all responsible for doing a small part of the work, and the entire system either communicates through a "command" server or directly with each other. Instead of building a supercomputer with thousands of computer parts, you can have 100 smaller computers that work with each other.

Some advantages include that anyone can participate as long as they have a computer/phone, it doesn't need special hardware, if one computer fails the rest of the system isn't affected, and it can be more powerful than the biggest supercomputers. Some disadvantages are the high cost if one person is paying for all the computers, and that not all tasks can be divided up among different computers (such as video game graphics and decoding videos).

Distributed programs are programs that are specifically designed to run on distributed computer systems. They're typically programs anyone can download, and after they're installed they automatically receive tasks and data from a command server coordinating everything. Some examples you may have heard of include SETI@Home (which analyzes radio signals for aliens), BOINC (a platform anyone can use to create distributed programs), and GIMPS (which looks for very large prime numbers). Folding@Home is another distributed computing project with a biomedical focus. So what does Folding@Home actually do?

Folding@Home is a distributed computing project that focuses on how proteins fold. This may not sound very exciting, but how proteins fold (or misfold) is deeply linked with diseases such as cancer, Alzheimer's, Ebola, Huntington's, and now COVID-19. Through Folding@Home, normal people can "donate" their computing power through a program which then allows scientists to run simulations on it. It helps scientists at every step of the process, from identifying possible drugs, testing them, and finally analyzing their effectiveness. Instead of spending millions on a few weeks of supercomputer time, scientists can split their simulation up into small "Work Units" individual computers can run, and spend the money more effectively. Here are some cool facts about Folding@Home:

  • It was developed as a collaboration by multiple universities, notably MIT and Stanford
  • It's had real-world results and led to many published papers
  • It was initially released in 2000, and users in the past have been able to fold on their smartphones, PS3s, and web browsers
  • Thanks to users around the world, it's now more powerful than the top 103 supercomputers worldwide—combined
  • Folding@Home is the largest distributed computing project, and it's bigger than all the other ones combined
  • Regardless of hardware, anyone can make the breakthrough that leads to a vaccine (but your chances may vary)

If this sounds exciting, you're probably wondering how you can help. Personally, I've been participating in this since 2017 and have completed quite a few Work Units. Here's how to set up everything and get started. If you need any help, shoot me an email.

  • Download the right installer for your operating system
  • After you install the program, choose any username to identify yourself on the leaderboards (your username, team number, and passkey has no bearing on the actual scientific research)
  • Google for a team number (many tech Youtube channels have teams and some computer companies give coupons if you join their team) or leave it blank
  • Get a passkey, since it gives you more points
  • Close the browser, and let it automatically run in the background. It's now processing data for scientists around the world!

FAQs without the questions: To avoid your computer slowing down, choose "Fold only when idle". Your computer's fan might speed up a little. To fight coronavirus, choose "I support research fighting any disease". If it says "waiting on server", it means there are so many people running Folding@Home the command server ran out of work (it'll automatically get work eventually). GPUs fold faster than CPUs, but they both help. DigitalOcean and Amazon EC2 instances work as well. Check your stats here.

Once again, email me if you need any help at all. Good luck, and have fun!
