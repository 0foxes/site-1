Folding@Home: How You Can Help Scientists Fight the COVID-19

24/03/2020

With an ever-increasing percentage of the world population under quarantine by the day (whether voluntarily or government-mandated), it's hard to avoid feeling at least a little bit helpless as scientists, doctors, and researchers around the world scramble to come up with treatments, vaccines, and models for the new coronavirus (COVID-19). What if you could do more than just stay at home? What if there was a way for you to actually help the scientists? There is.

Before we get into how to help with COVID-19 research, let's take a quick look at what distributed computing is. Essentially, it's a computer system that runs over multiple different computers. Those different computers are all responsible for doing a small part of the work, and the entire system either communicates through a "command" server or directly with each other. Instead of building a supercomputer with thousands of computer parts, you can have 100 smaller computers that work with each other.

Some advantages include that anyone can participate as long as they have a computer/phone, it doesn't need special hardware, if one computer fails the rest of the system isn't affected, and it can be more powerful than the biggest supercomputers. Some disadvantages are the high cost if one person is paying for all the computers, and that not all tasks can be divided up among different computers (such as video game graphics and decoding videos).

Distributed programs are programs that run on distributed computer systems. They're typically programs anyone can download, and after they're installed they automatically receive tasks and data from a command server coordinating everything. Some examples you may have heard of include SETI@Home (which analyzes radio signals for aliens), BOINC (a platform anyone can use to create distributed programs), and GIMPS (which looks for very large prime numbers). Folding@Home is another distributed computing project with a biomedical focus. So what does Folding@Home actually do?

Folding@Home is a distributed computing project that focuses on how proteins fold. This may not sound very exciting, but how proteins fold (or misfold) is deeply linked with diseases such as cancer, Alzheimer's, Ebola, and now COVID-19. Through Folding@Home, normal people can "donate" their computing power through a program, which then allows scientists to run simulations on it. Folding@Home helps scientists at every step of the process, from identifying possible drugs, testing them, and finally analyzing their effectiveness. Here are some fun facts about Folding@Home:

  • It was developed as a collaboration by multiple universities, notably MIT and Stanford
  • It's had proven results in the past
  • Thanks to users around the world, it's more powerful than the top 7 supercomputers worldwide—combined
  • Regardless of hardware, anyone can make the breakthrough that leads to a vaccine (but your chances vary)

If this sounds exciting, you're probably wondering how you can help. Personally, I've been participating in this since 2017 and have completed quite a few Work Units (simulations). Here's how to set up everything and get started. If you need any help, shoot me an email.

  • Download the right installer for your operating system
  • After you install, choose any username to identify yourself on the leaderboards
  • Google for a team number, or leave it blank (it doesn't matter much)
  • Get a passkey, since it gives you more points

FAQs without the questions: To avoid your computer slowing down, choose "Fold only when idle". Your computer's fan might speed up a little. To fight coronavirus, choose "I support research fighting any disease". If it says "waiting on server", it means there are so many people helping, the command server ran out of work (it'll automatically get work eventually). GPUs fold faster than CPUs, but they both help. DigitalOcean and Amazon EC2 instances work as well. Check your stats here.

Once again, shoot me an email if you need any help at all. Good luck, and have fun! (Hopefully a cure for Alzheimer's is discovered sometime in the next 75 years...)
